
# AI-Driven Displacement in Australia's Financial Services Sector: A Labour Market Policy Proposal

**To:** Deputy Secretary, Employment & Skills, Department of Employment and Workplace Relations
**From:** Hoang Bao Vinh
**Date:** April 2026
**Subject:** Evidence-based policy response to AI-driven labour displacement in financial services and administrative occupations

---

## Executive Summary

Generative AI is automating the routine cognitive tasks that underpin Australia's financial services and administrative workforce. Drawing on Acemoglu and Restrepo's (2018) task-displacement framework and Autor, Levy and Murnane's (2003) routinisation hypothesis, this proposal identifies financial services and clerical administration as the sector facing the most acute near-term displacement risk — with an estimated 35% of Australian jobs at high automation risk (Nedelkoska and Quintini 2018). Displacement falls disproportionately on women, regional workers, and mid-career employees. Importantly, the policy response must avoid regulatory interventions that suppress the productivity gains AI enables (Berg forthcoming). This proposal evaluates three distinct options — a national reskilling program, a wage insurance scheme, and a competition and data access policy — and recommends a sequenced package that preserves market dynamism while protecting the most vulnerable workers.

---

## Problem Definition & Context

The deployment of large language models (LLMs) in Australian financial institutions represents a structural shift, not a cyclical adjustment. Unlike earlier waves of automation that replaced physical labour, LLMs directly substitute for cognitive, data-intensive tasks — the core of financial services work. Eloundou et al. (2023) estimate that 80% of the US workforce faces LLM exposure of at least 10% of their tasks, with the effect concentrated in high-skill, high-wage occupations — a reversal of historical automation patterns. In Australia, Jobs and Skills Australia (2024) identifies bank tellers, administrative assistants, data entry operators, and insurance processors as occupations with greater than 50% task exposure to automation.

The economic mechanism is well-established. Acemoglu (2024) warns that AI may function as "so-so technology" — generating firm-level productivity gains while destroying more tasks than it creates, producing net labour income losses at the aggregate level. Australia's financial sector employed approximately 450,000 workers as of 2023 (ABS, 2024), with clerical and administrative roles comprising roughly 40% of that workforce. The absence of a dedicated labour market adjustment mechanism means displaced workers currently bear the full cost of transition.

---

## Analysis of Impacts

Following Autor, Levy and Murnane's (2003) task taxonomy, financial services work is dominated by routine cognitive tasks — data reconciliation, document processing, compliance checking, and customer query resolution — all of which face high LLM substitution risk. Non-routine interpersonal tasks such as complex financial advice and relationship management remain relatively protected, but represent a minority of positions sector-wide.

The wage and employment consequences are significant. Acemoglu and Restrepo (2018) demonstrate that each additional robot per 1,000 workers reduces the employment-to-population ratio by 0.2–0.3 percentage points and depresses wages by 0.4%. Applied to AI-driven cognitive task automation, these estimates imply material deterioration in labour market outcomes for mid-skill workers. Brynjolfsson, Li and Raymond (2023) offer a partial offset: GPT-4 access raised productivity among low-skill customer service workers by up to 35%, suggesting some displaced workers could transition into AI-augmented roles if adequately reskilled.

The distributional consequences are acute. Women represent approximately 70% of administrative and clerical workers in Australia (ABS, 2024), making this principally a gender equity issue. Workers aged 45 and above face the steepest retraining barriers due to lower baseline digital literacy and shorter remaining working lives over which to amortise reskilling investment. Regional workers face compounding disadvantage through limited access to alternative employment and training infrastructure. Without intervention, AI-driven displacement in financial services will deepen existing inequalities along gender, age, and geographic dimensions.

## Policy Options

| Option | Efficiency | Equity | Fiscal feasibility | Political feasibility | Implementation speed |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A: National reskilling program** | High — addresses structural skills gap | High — targets most vulnerable workers | Moderate — ~$2–3B over 5 years | High — bipartisan precedent (TAFE investment) | Slow — 18–36 months to employment outcomes |
| **B: Wage insurance scheme** | Moderate — reduces transition friction, not root cause | High — protects low-income displaced workers directly | Moderate — cost scales with displacement volume | High — builds on existing transfer system | Fast — operational within 6–12 months |
| **C: AI productivity levy** | High — corrects tax distortion favouring capital over labour | Moderate — indirect benefit via fund distribution | High — generates dedicated, self-sustaining revenue | Low — politically contested; industry opposition likely | Slow — requires primary legislation |

**Option A: National reskilling program**, modelled on Singapore's SkillsFuture initiative, would provide individual training accounts of AUD $2,000 per eligible worker, prioritising JSA-identified high-risk occupations and delivered through TAFE and industry-accredited micro-credential providers. OECD (2023) estimates active labour market programs return $1.50–$2.20 per dollar invested over five years. The primary limitation is a 18–36 month lag between program enrolment and employment outcomes; reskilling addresses labour supply but cannot guarantee demand for newly acquired credentials without parallel employer engagement.

**Option B: Wage insurance** would provide a subsidy equal to 50% of the wage gap for displaced workers who secure re-employment at a lower wage, capped at AUD $10,000 over two years. Katz and Krueger (2019) find wage insurance reduces unemployment duration by 15–20%. Option B is faster to deploy and more equitable in the short run than Option A, but addresses the symptoms of displacement rather than its structural causes, and risks entrenching workers in persistently lower-wage roles without a pathway to earnings recovery.

**Option C: AI productivity levy** would impose a 0.5% levy on AI-attributable productivity gains assessed at the firm level via existing ATO mechanisms, channelling revenue into a dedicated Labour Market Adjustment Fund. Acemoglu (2024) argues the current tax system systematically favours capital-augmenting technology over labour and that fiscal correction is necessary to realign investment incentives. Option C is theoretically the most efficient and fiscally self-sustaining intervention, but faces substantial political resistance from the business community and requires a multi-year legislative timeline before generating adjustment funding.

## Recommended Package & Implementation Plan

The government should implement Options A and B in sequence, with Option C introduced in Phase 3 to place the program on a sustainable fiscal footing.

| Phase | Timeline | Key actions | Lead agency | KPI |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1 — Foundation** | 0–6 months | Legislate wage insurance; establish eligibility via JSA displacement data | DEWR, Services Australia | Scheme operational; 10,000 workers identified and notified |
| **2 — Reskilling rollout** | 6–18 months | Launch individual training accounts; contract TAFE providers; pilot 3 micro-credential pathways in high-demand digital roles | Jobs and Skills Australia, DEWR | 25,000 workers enrolled; 80% credential completion rate |
| **3 — Levy & evaluation** | 18–36 months | Introduce AI productivity levy at 0.5%; evaluate re-employment outcomes; update training pathways using JSA demand forecasts | Treasury, ATO, Productivity Commission | Levy revenue exceeding $500M per annum; 60% of reskilled workers in equivalent or higher-wage employment within 24 months of program exit |

---

## Risks & Mitigations

**Risk 1 — Skills mismatch.** Reskilled workers may acquire credentials for occupations with insufficient employer demand, generating deadweight program expenditure. Mitigation: JSA must conduct quarterly forward-looking labour demand forecasting and gate training subsidies to credentials on a dynamic high-demand skills list, updated every six months in consultation with industry peak bodies.

**Risk 2 — Levy-induced capital flight.** Firms subject to the AI productivity levy may relocate AI operations or reduce Australian technology investment. Mitigation: Design the levy with a threshold exemption for firms below $50M annual revenue to protect SMEs, and pursue OECD-level coordination on minimum AI taxation standards to reduce cross-border arbitrage incentives — consistent with the multilateral approach adopted for the global minimum corporate tax.
