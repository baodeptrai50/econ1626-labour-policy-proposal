# AI-Driven Displacement in Australia's Financial Services Sector: A Labour Market Policy Proposal

**To:** Deputy Secretary, Employment & Skills, Department of Employment and Workplace Relations

**From:** Vinh Hoang Bao

**Date:** 10 May 2026

**Subject:** Evidence-based policy response to AI-driven labour displacement in financial services and administrative occupations

---

## Executive Summary
 
Generative AI is automating the routine cognitive tasks that underpin Australia's financial services and administrative workforce. Drawing on Acemoglu and Restrepo's (2018) task-displacement framework and Autor, Levy and Murnane's (2003) routinisation hypothesis, this proposal identifies financial services and clerical administration as the sector facing the most acute near-term displacement risk — with an estimated 35% of Australian jobs at high automation risk (Nedelkoska and Quintini 2018). Displacement falls disproportionately on women, regional workers, and mid-career employees. Importantly, the policy response must avoid regulatory interventions that suppress the productivity gains AI enables (Berg forthcoming). This proposal evaluates three distinct options — a national reskilling program, a wage insurance scheme, and a competition and data access policy — and recommends a sequenced package that preserves market dynamism while protecting the most vulnerable workers.
 
---
 
## Problem Definition & Context
 
The deployment of large language models (LLMs) in Australian financial institutions represents a structural shift, not a cyclical adjustment. Unlike earlier automation waves that substituted for physical labour, LLMs directly substitute for cognitive and data-intensive tasks — the core of financial services work. Eloundou et al. (2023) estimate that 80% of the US workforce faces LLM exposure across at least 10% of their tasks, with concentration in high-skill, high-wage occupations — a reversal of historical automation patterns. In Australia, Jobs and Skills Australia (2024) identifies bank tellers, administrative assistants, data entry operators, and insurance processors as occupations with greater than 50% task exposure.
 
The core economic risk is what Acemoglu (2024) terms "so-so technology": AI that generates firm-level productivity gains while automating tasks faster than new labour-demanding tasks are created, producing net labour income losses at the aggregate level. Australia's financial sector employed approximately 450,000 workers in 2023 (ABS 2024), with clerical and administrative roles comprising roughly 40% of that workforce. The policy challenge is to manage transitional displacement without suppressing the broader productivity gains that AI-enabled financial services will generate for the Australian economy.
 
---
 
## Analysis of Impacts
 
Following Autor, Levy and Murnane's (2003) task taxonomy, financial services work is dominated by routine cognitive tasks — data reconciliation, document processing, compliance checking, and customer query resolution — all of which face high LLM substitution risk. Non-routine interpersonal tasks such as complex financial advice and relationship management remain comparatively protected but represent a minority of positions sector-wide.
 
Acemoglu and Restrepo (2018) demonstrate that each additional robot per 1,000 workers reduces the employment-to-population ratio by 0.2–0.3 percentage points and depresses wages by 0.4%. Applied to AI-driven cognitive task automation, these estimates imply material deterioration in labour market outcomes for mid-skill workers. Brynjolfsson, Li and Raymond (2023) offer a partial offset: GPT-4 access raised productivity among low-skill customer service workers by up to 35%, suggesting that workers who gain access to AI tools — rather than being displaced by them — can achieve substantial productivity gains. This finding has direct policy implications: widening access to AI tools, rather than restricting their deployment, is the more efficient path to equity.
 
The distributional consequences are nonetheless acute. Women represent approximately 70% of administrative and clerical workers in Australia (ABS 2024), making this principally a gender equity concern. Workers aged 45 and above face the steepest retraining barriers due to lower baseline digital literacy and shorter working lives over which to amortise reskilling investment. Regional workers face compounding disadvantage through limited access to alternative employment and training infrastructure.
 
At the sector level, financial services is not contracting but restructuring: routine processing roles are being consolidated or eliminated while demand grows for hybrid roles combining domain expertise with AI oversight capabilities — compliance analysts, AI-augmented financial advisers, and model risk officers. Jobs and Skills Australia (2024) identifies these emerging roles as among the fastest-growing in the sector, yet the skills pipeline remains underdeveloped. The policy implication is that displacement and skills shortage coexist within the same sector simultaneously — a structural mismatch that market forces alone will not resolve within the adjustment window available to displaced workers.

## Policy Options
 
| Option | Efficiency | Equity | Fiscal feasibility | Political feasibility | Implementation speed |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A: National reskilling program** | High — addresses structural skills gap | High — targets most vulnerable workers | Moderate — ~$2–3B over 5 years | High — bipartisan precedent (TAFE investment) | Slow — 18–36 months to employment outcomes |
| **B: Wage insurance scheme** | Moderate — reduces transition friction, not root cause | High — protects low-income displaced workers directly | Moderate — cost scales with displacement volume | High — builds on existing transfer system | Fast — operational within 6–12 months |
| **C: Competition & data access policy** | High — broadens AI productivity gains across firms and workers | Moderate — indirect benefit via market structure | Low direct cost — regulatory mechanism | Moderate — tension with IP and incumbent interests | Slow — requires legislative change |
 
**Option A: National reskilling program**, modelled on Singapore's SkillsFuture initiative, would provide individual training accounts of AUD $2,000 per eligible worker, prioritising JSA-identified high-risk occupations and delivered through TAFE and industry-accredited micro-credential providers. OECD (2023) estimates active labour market programs return $1.50–$2.20 per dollar invested over five years. Critically, program design should be portable and worker-owned rather than employer-administered, avoiding the monopsony problem that arises when firms control reskilling access. The primary limitation is an 18–36 month lag between enrolment and employment outcomes. Option A is more structurally effective than Option B but less fiscally immediate — it addresses the root cause of displacement rather than cushioning its short-term income effects, making it the stronger long-run investment of the two.
 
**Option B: Wage insurance** would provide a subsidy equal to 50% of the wage gap for displaced workers who secure re-employment at a lower wage, capped at AUD $10,000 over two years. Hyman, Kovak and Leive (2024) find that wage insurance reduces non-employment duration by approximately one calendar quarter and increases the probability of re-employment by 8–17 percentage points within two years — with cumulative earnings recoveries exceeding AUD $27,000 over four years, making the scheme largely self-financing through returned tax revenue. Option B is faster to deploy than Option A and more equitable in the short run, but addresses transitional displacement rather than its structural causes. There is a design risk that wage insurance entrench workers in lower-wage roles without providing a pathway to earnings recovery. Option B is more equitable in the short run than Option C — which delivers only indirect benefits through market structure reform — but less efficient than Option A at closing the structural skills gap; it should therefore be paired with Option A, not used as a standalone intervention.
 
**Option C: Competition and data access policy** would mandate interoperability of AI systems used in financial services and open access to non-personal training datasets held by incumbent institutions, enabling smaller firms and new entrants to deploy competitive AI tools. The OECD (2023) AI Policy Observatory identifies concentrated AI development among a small number of frontier providers as a source of emerging monopsony dynamics in digital labour markets. As Berg (forthcoming) argues, a classical liberal approach to AI governance prioritises access and competition over redistribution: if workers and smaller firms can access the same productivity-enhancing AI tools as large incumbents, the distributional case for heavier interventions is substantially weakened. Option C is the least fiscally costly option but carries regulatory complexity and a long legislative lead time. Compared to Options A and B, it delivers no direct income support to displaced workers and operates on a reform horizon incompatible with the immediate adjustment needs of the workforce; it is best understood as a structural complement to, not a substitute for, direct labour market interventions.

## Recommended Package & Implementation Plan
 
The government should implement Options A and B in sequence, with Option C initiated concurrently to address the market structure conditions that determine long-run equity outcomes.
 
| Phase | Timeline | Key actions | Lead agency | KPI |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1 — Foundation** | 0–6 months | Legislate wage insurance; establish eligibility via JSA displacement data; begin competition policy scoping review | DEWR, Services Australia, ACCC | Scheme operational; 10,000 workers identified and notified; scoping review published |
| **2 — Reskilling rollout** | 6–18 months | Launch individual training accounts; contract TAFE providers; pilot 3 micro-credential pathways in high-demand digital roles | Jobs and Skills Australia, DEWR | 25,000 workers enrolled; 80% credential completion rate |
| **3 — Market reform** | 18–36 months | Introduce data interoperability obligations for financial services AI; evaluate employment outcomes; update training pathways using JSA demand forecasts | Treasury, ACCC, Productivity Commission | 3 interoperability standards enacted; 60% of reskilled workers in equivalent or higher-wage employment within 24 months of program exit |

## Risks & Mitigations
 
**Risk 1 — Skills mismatch.** Reskilled workers may acquire credentials for occupations with insufficient employer demand, generating deadweight program expenditure. Mitigation: JSA must conduct quarterly forward-looking labour demand forecasting and gate training subsidies to a dynamic high-demand skills list, updated every six months in consultation with industry. Program design should be responsive to market signals, not supply-driven.
 
**Risk 2 — Regulatory overreach in competition policy.** Poorly scoped data interoperability mandates could impose compliance costs that deter AI investment, reducing the aggregate productivity gains that make labour adjustment affordable. Mitigation: The ACCC should conduct a cost-benefit analysis prior to any mandate, with a minimum revenue threshold exempting small and medium enterprises from interoperability obligations. Regulation should be targeted and evidence-based, consistent with the principle that policy intervention is justified only where market failures are clearly identified and proportionate responses exist (Berg forthcoming).
